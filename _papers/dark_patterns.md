title: "Designing Consent&#58; Choice Architecture and Consumer Welfare in Data Sharing"
abstract: "We study how cookie-consent design affects consumer welfare using a framed field experiment that randomizes consent banners. We examine three margins: visual nudges, defaults when users dismiss banners, and whether consent is set at the website or browser level. Visual changes have little effect, but deliberate obstruction such as hiding \"reject all\" substantially shifts choices. Because many users close banners, welfare depends on both the website default and users' beliefs about defaults. We estimate a model of consumer choice. The model estimates show that when consent is requested on a site-by-site basis, the optimal design eliminates obstructions and aligns defaults with the majority preference. Relative to this optimum, typical U.S. consent practices cost 20% of surplus through obstruction, and typical EU consent practices decrease surplus by 6%. Correcting users' beliefs about website default raises surplus by 15% compared to the optimal site-by-site consent. The largest welfare gains, however, come from replacing site-by-site consent with browser-level consent, which helps consumers avoid the time costs of making repeated consent decisions."
