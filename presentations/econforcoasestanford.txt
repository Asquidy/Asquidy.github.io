# Slide(4):

TopTitle: Statements about AI agents in the economy
TopText.8:
1. They will participate as laborers doing productive work. 
2. They will act as personal agents engaging in the market on our behalf.
3. They will collaborate, coordinate, and compete in a variety of ways, just like humans do. 
4. Coase's insights hold; transaction costs will dictate the organizational structure for both humans and agents.
5. We will need rules, tools, and market design innovations to make this new world work well.

TopTitle: Example: Claude code

TopText:
<<<
- Markdown files specify persistent memory and usage patterns.
- The main instance determines when to spawn sub-agents (for context window protection, parallelization, task isolation, and specialization).

- The process is a while loop that calls sub-agents of depth one.

# Slide(22):

TopTitle: Analogy for AI agents

TopText.8:
<<<
- What are agents' costs? The number of tokens used in completing the task.
- What is success? Does the agent succeed in completing the task.
- Do agents have private information? Perhaps, if they have context or if they can "introspect."
- What is reputation? Doesn't exist for each unique instance.
- What are preferences? Unclear.
<<<

# Slide(23):

TopTitle: Production functions for AI agents

TopText.8:
<<<

- We don't have an understanding of which one is chosen.
<<<

# Slide(31):

TopTitle: Market based scaffolding for AI agents

TopText.8:
<<<
- There is a queue of tasks to be completed.
- Each workers submits a bid for a task.
- A bid is an ask, self assessment of probability of success, and expected completion time (currently not used).
- For each bid, the engine computes: P(Success) x Reward - Bid - E[Cost] - (1-P(Success)) x Penalty
- All bids with Score >= 0 are considered, and the one with the highest score is awarded the task first unless otherwise occupied.
- If failure, next highest score is considered.
<<<

TopTitle: Tradeoffs of "bowling shoe" agents

TopText.8:
<<<
- They will presumably be better with their own data and apis.
- Concern about agent bias or self-preferencing.

- Computational costs shift to the platform, though it could charge for them.
- "Do I really want Walmart to know my WTP?"
- Liability shifts to the platform, and security may be easier to manage centrally.
<<<

- Can be used across platforms, potentially making them more capable.

# Slide(46):

TopTitle: Equilibrium possibilities: the good place

TopText.8:
<<<
- Improved preference elicitation and lower search costs improve taste matching.
- Better matching strengthens incentives for firms to invest in quality.
- Advertising may involve less puffery, especially with stronger reputation systems.
- In bargaining, AI lowers the opportunity cost of negotiating; negotiations can start earlier and persist longer, leading to better outcomes.
- Firms have less scope to extract rents from behavioral biases and bounded rationality.
<<<

# Slide(47):

TopTitle: The bad place, robot ripoff hell
TopText.8:
<<<
- Prices and dispersion may not necessarily go down:
  - If products are (horizontally) differentiated, better matching can lead to higher prices and dispersion
- Firms may use obfuscation techniques to preserve or increase rents (e.g., bundling unrelated products in the offer to confuse/overload user's AI agent).
- Zero-sum games may lead to race to the bottom, or arms race to exploit rents from having a “better” agent.
- Markets with externalities: e.g., congestion in AI-assisted job applications
<<<

# Slide(48):

TopTitle: Getting to the good place
TopText.8:
<<<
Matching:
- Deferred Acceptance at scale (e.g., dating apps, online labor markets)
- Multiple "rounds" can happen in milliseconds
- Extensive (but lightening-fast) rounds of negotiation and information transmission
Privacy-preserving queries:
  - Agents ask questions from employers without signaling (e.g., asking about maternity leave)
Negotiation protocols:
  - Fixed windows, no exploding offers, programmable holds.
<<<

# Slide(49):

TopTitle: Preventing the bad place
TopText.8:
<<<
- Costly signaling:
  - Journal submission, job applications, etc. need to be costly.
- Proof of humanity and proof of agent ownership.
  - Need to be able to prove that you are human and that you own the agent.
- Congestion taxes and rationing mechanisms. 
  - Cloudflare's pay per crawl.
- Bring your own agent.
  - Acts on your behalf and helps you not to be exploited.
- Clear liability rules.
<<<
